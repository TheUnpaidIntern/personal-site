Unpaid Intern

A synthesiser for the industrial design process

The Unpaid Intern was my graduation project at CSM in 2015 - imagine a synthesizer for 3D models. Drop in any CAD file and explore the design space through intuitive controls, just like a musician shaping sound. The interface lets you save presets and collaborate naturally, without everyone needing to huddle around a computer. Say your team wants to capture the feeling of midwest emo in product form - you can use the tool to explore what details make something feel like Never Meant by American Football. Save that direction as a preset, amplify those characteristics or tone them down, blend it with post-punk influences, then hand the controls to your colleagues to explore further without needing CAD expertise. Like working with a helpful intern, it takes your vague creative direction and returns with countless variations, ready for feedback and refinement. While most AI tools at the time were trying to take over the entire creative process, I wanted to explore how algorithms could enhance rather than replace traditional design workflows.

This project opened up a path I hadn't expected. Back in 2015, I was focused on industrial design - dreaming about working at places like Barber Osgerby. But when everyone started calling this an 'interaction design' project, I figured I should probably learn what that meant. Looking at what was coming out of the RCA at the time, especially through Durrell Bishop's influence, I realized that was exactly the direction I needed to go. While I had these ideas about generative tools and AI augmentation, I barely knew how to prototype them - coding wasn't something I'd really explored. The concept of collaborating with AI wasn't new even then, but it's evolved enormously since 2015. Looking back, this project started me down a rabbit hole of exploring human-AI collaboration in design, one that I'm still following today.
